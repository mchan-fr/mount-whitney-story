Hiking
Among
The Clouds

We expected a long trek to the top of Mount Whitney.
What we got was a grueling footslog that pushed us to our limits.
By Marcus Chan
Lone Pine, California | September 20, 2025

When we woke up in the wee hours of our big day, we saw something we hadn't seen in days: clear skies.

It had been nonstop wet weather, and just a day earlier, we had heard that hikers were forced to turn around near the top of Mount Whitney because of blizzard conditions. It's not what you want to hear the day before your own attempt.

This was an important hike for us. My daughter is named after the mountain, and we'd always hoped to reach the summit together. (Before I had kids, I reached the top twice.)

We trained for months, subjecting ourselves to long, steep hikes on ankle-twisting terrain with packs filled with weights. But we knew that the weather would have the final say.

So to wake up to this view was promising. Energized, my daughter, son and I began our ascent to 14,505 feet.

2:32 a.m. (half an hour into the hike)

There's nothing technically difficult about the main trail when it's free of snow and ice. Still, you do have to watch almost every step you take, especially at night.

There's little risk of falling off a cliff during the early parts of the hike. But when you climb above 12,000 feet, the drop-offs become steep, the altitude can make you weak and dizzy, and the trail exposes you to strong winds and severe weather.

(Recently, two hikers on this trail fell off a cliff. One suffered traumatic brain injury, but survived. The other did not.)

We've just begun our hike, so we're only around 8,800 feet up at this point. Our focus is navigating the darkness.

(To hear the rushing water, unmute in the lower left corner.)

6:04 a.m. (4 hours into the hike)

Daylight comes. After hiking for hours by headlamp, the rising sun reveals the hard, jagged, beautiful landscape we've been traversing. We're close to 11,000 feet. I feel like I've been transported.

So far, we've been moving at a steady pace, but the higher we go, the slower we'll go. Griffin is a faster hiker, but it's important for Whitney to go at a pace that's sustainable. The weather may have the final say, but the altitude can have as loud a voice.

Far off in the distance (upper left corner of the photo), we catch our first glimpse of the famous golden hue (or "alpenglow") of the main Mount Whitney massif.

That's around 13,000 feet. It's where we're headed.

7:26 a.m. (5-1/2 hours into the hike)

The clear skies didn't last for long.

A sea of clouds slowly creeps up behind us. We're at an elevation of around 11,700 feet.

The sun is out, yet it feels colder. The altitude is making us breathe a little harder.

We run into only one other person on this leg of the trail. It's a tall, young guy who passes us, but then takes a wrong turn, and ends up behind us.

Even though this is a well-worn trail, it's not always obvious which way to go. That's why we have GPS-enabled maps on our phones (I'm using FarOut).

8:37 a.m. (6-1/2 hours into the hike)

We make it to Trail Camp, which is above 12,000 feet. For context, we started our hike at just under 8,400 feet.

When you're above 8,000 feet, the Cleveland Clinic says you shouldn't ascend more than 1,640 feet per day. We've already doubled that amount. If we reach the summit, we'll more than triple the recommended daily limit.

We're 6 miles in with 5 more to the top. And it's a hard 5, starting with a section known as the "99 switchbacks." It's more than 2 miles long and ascends around 1,800 feet. It can become dangerous when there's snow or ice on it (we packed micro-spikes just in case).

We take a short breather by a small lake and gird ourselves for what's to come.

We take a break by the tarn next to Trail Camp.
Whitney and Griffin on the 99 Switchbacks.
The clouds stay at bay for now.

12:45 p.m. (almost 11 hours into the hike)

We reach Trail Crest at 13,654 feet. It's significant on several fronts.

First, it marks the end of the 99 switchbacks and the start of the final and toughest leg: 2.8 miles to the summit.

Second, this is where blizzard conditions the day before forced hikers to abandon their summit bid. As you can see, there's little protection from storms or strong winds along the narrow ridge.

Thus far, the weather has been perfect. (Unmute in the lower left to hear the light wind.)

And third, this is roughly where Whitney and I turned back last year during our our first attempt. On that occasion, the altitude was making Whitney feel ill. She was determined to push on, but I didn't think it was safe, so we descended.

Today was a big improvement.

Don't Look Down

Remember when I said there was little risk of falling off a cliff until you're above 12,000 feet?

This is what I meant. The video was taken about 4 feet from the middle of the trail. There are many steep drop-offs like this along the last leg.

On top of that, if you see dark clouds coming, this sign becomes more important. That hissing (bullet #3) may not be a snake.

Higher and Slower

For those who have never felt the effects of high altitudes, hiking another 2.8 miles can feel insurmountable, especially when the terrain seems determined to wreck your legs.

Each step takes effort and the breathing only gets harder since you're above 13,500 feet the rest of the way.

Meanwhile, your brain tells you that you've gone too far to turn back, just as your body insists you still have much too far to go.

There's a delicate balance of pushing yourself to the limits but not endangering yourself or the group.

Mt. Whitney's 'Windows'

Most of the time along the high, narrow ridge, you have the security of a rock wall to your right in case you need to lean against something. But there are a number of short sections known as "windows" where there are no walls, and you feel a bit like you're walking a tightrope.

Granted, it's a tightrope that's roughly 6 feet wide, but if you're already feeling unsteady because of fatigue, it can be a little precarious. It doesn't help that strong gusts of wind can blow through these windows.

3:45 p.m. (almost 14 hours into the hike)

Whitney gives a thumbs-up, despite the fatigue caused by the altitude. She is digging deep into her well of resilience to reach the summit. We're at 14,047 feet with still about half a mile to go.

For the past mile or so, we've forced ourselves not to think about the total distance left. Instead, we focused only on the next 0.1 mile to cover. (The bigger the challenge, the smaller the pieces you need to chunk the challenge into.)

Every couple hundred steps or so, I check my GPS map and give an update on whether we covered that 0.1 mile. Each time we do, it gives us a sense of accomplishment and fuels the next 0.1. This approach keeps us moving forward.

4:53 p.m. (15 hours into the hike)

We see the hut. Officially known as the Smithsonian Institution Shelter, it represents the top of Mount Whitney. The finish line is now within view.

We run into Griffin who has already summited and is now descending. For much of the hike, he's had to fight his urge to move faster. So during the final mile, I tell him to go on ahead. I know the general rule is to stay together, but the path is straightforward at this point.

We summit at 4:58 p.m.

We reach the shelter. Whitney did it! As the video shows, it's a moment of extreme pride and pure exhaustion.

Another proud papa moment. It's the first fourteener for Whitney and Griffin, and my third time to the top of this mountain.

This is no easy feat. One of my friends who I summited with years ago recently confided in me that he quietly cried when he reached the top.

I allow ourselves a moment to literally bask in the sun and the moment. But it's also late in the day to be at the summit. Fast hikers would already be finished with the 22-mile trek.

So we take our well-earned summit photos, leave our name on the wall of names and sign the registry. And then our focus quickly turns to our descent.

5:49 p.m. (almost 16 hours into the hike)

We begin our descent and try to cover as much ground as we can before the sun goes down. Once we lose light, trails can disappear and the chances of something bad happening increase greatly.

The clouds begin to reach us, but I'm amazed at how calm they are. The winds continue to softly blow. Everything is so peaceful. We were so lucky â€” the weather was perfect.

As the sun begins to set, the colors change from a soft white to an amber to a cool blue-gray. Mother Nature puts on a stunning light show. I wish we could enjoy it, but I know darkness is coming.

7:27 p.m. (17-1/2 hours into the hike)

Our headlamps are on again. We're not close to the 99 switchbacks.

The altitude starts to hit me. Even though we've been descending for more than an hour, we're still above 13,500 feet. I have a slight headache and feel cold, even though I have five layers on.

At one point my kids tell me to rest, but I tell them that the only thing that's going to make me feel better is descending. At this point, 11,000 sounds really good.

1:31 a.m. (almost 24 hours into the hike)

For us mere mortals, doing Mount Whitney in one day is going to be a long day no matter what. But you're preparing for 15 or 16 hours of continuous hiking, not 24-plus hours. That's nuts. But that's what happened.

As we descended below 13,000 feet, Whitney and Griffin seemed to get stronger. But I started to run out of gas. At one point I lost my balance from exhaustion and fell to the ground. Weirdly, I sort of just bounced right back up. So we kept moving.

We made a couple of wrong turns, which added to our time and fatigue.

This is one of the few photos taken during the last 8 miles of the hike because we were focused on only one thing: getting off this mountain.

6:42 a.m. (almost 29 hours into the hike)

Epilogue

You know it was a long day when you got to see the sun rise twice during the same hike.

When we reached our car, we saw other folks walking around the portal. Their day was just beginning. Ours was finally ending. We hadn't slept in more than a day.

For each of us, it was an intense experience that was both humbling and confidence-building. This was physically and mentally the hardest thing any of us have faced. This wasn't the challenge we prepared for nor expected, but it was the challenge we got. And we rose to it.

All 14,505 feet of it.
